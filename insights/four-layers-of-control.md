> This article is adapted from an Alto Ridge Advisory social post on control strategies.

In regulated environments (GMP/GxP), a control strategy isn’t just a diagram in a slide deck – it’s how you reliably get from “intended process” to “real-world execution” batch after batch.

## The Four Layers of Control

Building a strong control strategy starts with understanding its layers.  
At Alto Ridge, we group controls into four practical layers that work well across biologics, sterile fill/finish, and other GMP operations:

1. **Material controls** – specs, CoA review, supplier qualification, incoming inspection  
2. **Process controls** – CPPs/CMAs, in-process checks, alarms, and trend review  
3. **Equipment controls** – calibration, maintenance, cleaning/sterilization, automated interlocks  
4. **People controls** – training, qualification, behaviors, and documented roles

## Practical Takeaways

For any critical risk or process step:

- **Put at least one control in each layer** (Material, Process, Equipment, People).  
- **Define what “failure” looks like per layer** (e.g., out of spec, missed check, overdue PM, untrained operator).  
- **Assign owners and review cadence** so each layer has someone clearly accountable.  
- **Keep evidence easy to retrieve** (batches, logs, training records, deviations, CAPAs) for when questions come up or inspectors arrive.

A robust control strategy isn’t just a slide—it’s a living system that makes day-to-day decisions easier, keeps you inspection-ready, and gives you a clear story when something goes wrong.
